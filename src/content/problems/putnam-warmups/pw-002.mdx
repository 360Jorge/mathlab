## Problem
Start with the number $1$. You may repeatedly apply either of the following operations:
- replace $n$ by $n + 3$, or
- replace $n$ by $2n$.

Is it possible to reach $5$?

---

## Hints
### Hint 1
Try working modulo a small number.

### Hint 2
Check how each operation behaves modulo $3$.

---

## Solution
We examine all numbers modulo $3$.

- The operation $n \mapsto n + 3$ does not change the value of $n \bmod 3$.
- The operation $n \mapsto 2n$ sends $n \bmod 3$ to $2n \bmod 3$.

Starting from $1$, the possible residues modulo $3$ are:
$$
1 \xrightarrow{\times 2} 2 \xrightarrow{\times 2} 1,
$$
so the only reachable residues are $1$ and $2$ modulo $3$.

However,
$$
5 \equiv 2 \pmod{3},
$$
so the modular condition alone does not rule it out.

Now check actual reachability:
- From $1$, doubling gives $2$, then doubling gives $4$.
- From $4$, adding $3$ gives $7$, then $10$, etc.

No sequence of allowed operations produces $5$.

Thus, it is **not possible** to reach $5$.
