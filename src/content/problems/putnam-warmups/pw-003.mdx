## Problem
Let $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{11}$ be integers chosen from the set
$$
\{1, 2, 3, \dots, 20\}.
$$
Show that there exist two distinct indices $i \neq j$ such that
$$
|a_i - a_j| \le 1.
$$

---

## Hints

### Nudge
You are choosing many numbers from a *small interval* (1 to 20).  
Try organizing the integers into small groups before thinking about specific values.

### Focus
Instead of looking at the chosen numbers, partition the entire set $\{1,\dots,20\}$ into pieces where numbers inside each piece are close together.

How many such pieces can you make if each piece contains numbers whose difference is at most $1$?

### Key idea
There are only finitely many groups, but you are selecting more numbers than groups.  
What must happen to at least two of the chosen integers?

### Finish
If two chosen integers land in the same group, what does that immediately tell you about their difference?


---

## Solution
Partition the set $\{1,2,\dots,20\}$ into the following $10$ pairs:
$$
\{1,2\}, \{3,4\}, \dots, \{19,20\}.
$$

Each pair contains two integers whose difference is at most $1$.

Since we choose $11$ integers but there are only $10$ such pairs, by the pigeonhole principle, at least two of the chosen integers must lie in the same pair.

Therefore, there exist indices $i \neq j$ such that
$$
|a_i - a_j| \le 1.
$$

This completes the proof.
